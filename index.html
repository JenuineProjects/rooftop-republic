How This Solution Addresses the Problem

The brief identifies a specific perception problem. Here is how our Urban Farming Expert diagnosed the barriers and how the landing page systematically dismantles each one.

The Problem
"People think urban farming sounds nice but assume it requires expertise, time, and equipment they don't have."
The Three Assumed Barriers
Barrier 1
"I need expertise"
People picture someone with a horticulture degree and years of experience. They think there's a large body of knowledge they need before they can even start. They don't realise that 90% of community garden members started knowing absolutely nothing.
Barrier 2
"I don't have time"
People overestimate how much time a rooftop garden demands and underestimate how little is actually needed. A small container setup needs just 20-30 minutes twice a week in growing season. That's less time than scrolling social media.
Barrier 3
"I need equipment"
People imagine expensive raised beds, specialist tools, and professional setups. In reality, you can start with a recycled container, a €4 bag of compost, and a €2 packet of seeds. Total cost to start: under €10.
The Deeper Barrier
"This isn't for people like me"
Before anyone even considers expertise, time, or equipment, they've already decided whether this is "for people like me." This identity barrier is the single biggest obstacle. The founder captured it perfectly: they need to think "I could do that" instead of "that's not for me."
How the Landing Page Dismantles Each Barrier
Breaking the Identity Barrier: "This IS for people like me"
The landing page shows diverse, relatable Dubliners who started as complete beginners. The micro-stories feature a tech worker in Phibsborough, a Polish construction worker in Smithfield, a retired widow in Stoneybatter, and a young family in Rathmines. The bold identity shift statement ("You don't need to know anything about gardening. You don't need a big rooftop. You don't even need a rooftop.") directly confronts the "not for me" feeling. Every image, name, and neighbourhood is Dublin-specific so visitors see themselves on the page.
Page sections: Identity Shift, Micro-Stories, Dublin Map • Core Drives: CD1 (Epic Meaning), CD5 (Social Influence)
Dismantling "I Need Expertise"
Instead of a guide or manual, the page offers a single, impossibly simple first step: the Herb Pot Challenge. Plant one pot of herbs this weekend. 15 minutes. Under €10. No knowledge required — just fill a pot with compost, plant herbs, put it outside, water when dry. The interactive "What Can I Grow?" quiz gives personalised recommendations so visitors don't need to research anything. The "Yeah, but..." accordion addresses the expertise objection head-on: "Outdoor plants have rain, natural light, and soil biology on their side. Outdoor growing is more forgiving than you think."
Page sections: Herb Pot Challenge, Quiz, "Yeah but..." Accordion • Core Drives: CD2 (Accomplishment), CD3 (Creativity & Feedback)
Dismantling "I Don't Have Time"
The Herb Pot Challenge anchors the time commitment at 15 minutes this Saturday — not "hours per week" or "ongoing commitment." The page explicitly states that a container garden needs just 20 minutes twice a week. The Growing Journey progression shows that you start tiny and scale only when you want to. Community meetups are presented as monthly and optional. The seasonal timeline normalises winter as a quiet period ("You haven't failed because nothing is growing in January"), preventing the guilt of seasonal disengagement.
Page sections: Herb Pot Challenge, Growing Journey, Seasonal Timeline • Core Drives: CD2 (Accomplishment), CD6 (Scarcity — seasonal windows)
Dismantling "I Need Equipment"
The Herb Pot Challenge specifies exactly what you need: one pot (even an old saucepan), a €4 bag of compost, and a €2 herb plant or seed packet. Total: under €10. The page avoids any imagery of professional gardening equipment, raised bed systems, or specialist tools. The micro-stories reinforce this: Niamh started with a €1.99 Tesco basil plant. The "Yeah, but..." section addresses cost directly with honest numbers, not vague reassurances. The quiz results recommend plants that work in simple containers.
Page sections: Herb Pot Challenge, "Yeah but..." Accordion, Quiz Results • Core Drives: CD3 (Creativity & Feedback), CD4 (Ownership)
The Core Strategy: Make the First Step Inevitable

The landing page does not try to convince visitors that urban farming is important, or that they should commit to a rooftop garden. It does one thing: make the very first, very small step feel so achievable that not doing it feels like the bigger effort.

This follows the Urban Farming Expert's "First Step Strategy" — a staircase from a €2 supermarket basil plant to running a full rooftop. The landing page is responsible for making Step 1 feel inevitable and Step 2 (sharing a photo, attending a drop-in) feel inviting. Everything after that, the community handles.

The gamification framework reinforces this through urgent optimism (Jane McGonigal): the combination of believing you can succeed and wanting to act immediately. White Hat drives (meaning, accomplishment, creativity) build the belief. Targeted Black Hat moments (seasonal scarcity, community momentum) create the urgency. The result: "I could do that, and I want to start this weekend."

How This Solution Meets the 8 Core Drives

Yu-kai Chou's Octalysis Framework identifies 8 Core Drives behind all human motivation. This landing page was designed to activate each drive intentionally. Below is how each core drive is embedded into the page, with specific examples of where visitors experience it.

CD1
Epic Meaning & Calling
White Hat • High Priority
Visitors feel they are joining something greater than themselves. Rooftop Republic is not a gardening service — it is a movement to transform Dublin's unused rooftops into a citywide food network.
Where on the page: The hero headline ("Dublin Has Thousands of Empty Rooftops. We're Turning Them Into Gardens") frames a city-scale mission. The collective goal tracker ("420 kg of 1,000 kg grown this year") ties individual action to communal impact. The final CTA ("Be Part of the Next 160") positions the visitor as joining a movement, not completing a form.
CD2
Development & Accomplishment
White Hat • High Priority
Visitors see a clear progression path from absolute beginner to experienced grower, with meaningful milestones at each stage. Progress feels earned, not given.
Where on the page: The Growing Journey section visualises 6 stages from "Plant a Herb Pot" to "Lead a Rooftop." The Herb Pot Challenge provides a concrete first achievement (grow herbs this weekend for under €10 in 15 minutes). The animated counter stats (40 rooftops, 250+ growers) show community-level accomplishment. The "You are here" badge on Step 1 sets the visitor at the start of their journey.
CD3
Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback
White Hat • High Priority (Golden Corner)
The "Golden Corner" of Octalysis — White Hat and intrinsically motivated. Visitors engage in creative exploration with immediate feedback, making the experience inherently rewarding.
Where on the page: The "What Can I Grow?" interactive quiz lets visitors input their space, experience, and preferences and instantly receive personalised plant recommendations. This is an evergreen mechanic — visitors create their own outcome. The seasonal timeline shows what's possible right now, empowering informed choices. The Herb Pot Challenge lets visitors pick their own herb and approach.
CD4
Ownership & Possession
Middle Drive • Medium Priority
Visitors begin to feel ownership before they've even signed up. The language and framing makes the rooftop feel like "theirs" — activating the endowment effect.
Where on the page: The headline "What's Your Rooftop Doing Right Now?" triggers psychological ownership over a space the visitor already has. The quiz results feel personalised ("Your rooftop garden could grow..."). The Dublin neighbourhood map lets visitors find "their" local community. The CTA "Claim Your Rooftop" uses ownership language. The Save/Share buttons on the Herb Pot Challenge create a sense of bookmarking something that's "mine."
CD5
Social Influence & Relatedness
Middle Drive • High Priority
People join because they see people like themselves already doing it. The community IS the product. Social proof, relatedness, and belonging are woven throughout.
Where on the page: The micro-stories section (Niamh in Phibsborough, Tomasz in Smithfield, Margaret in Stoneybatter, Roisin in Rathmines) shows diverse, relatable Dubliners who started as beginners. The animated counters (250+ growers) provide social proof through numbers. The Dublin map shows real neighbourhoods with active communities. The identity shift statement ("You don't need to know anything about gardening") uses social normalisation to dismantle the "not for me" barrier. Cooperative framing ("Together we're growing Dublin's food") over competitive language throughout.
CD6
Scarcity & Impatience
Black Hat • Low Priority (Used Sparingly)
Used lightly and honestly — only where real scarcity exists. No artificial urgency or false countdown timers.
Where on the page: The seasonal timeline creates real, natural scarcity ("Plant peas now — the window is March to May"). The gap between 40 current rooftops and 200 needed implies limited early-adopter positions. The "next drop-in" dates on neighbourhood cards create genuine appointment dynamics. These are all real constraints, not manufactured urgency — consistent with the 80/20 White Hat / Black Hat balance.
CD7
Unpredictability & Curiosity
Black Hat • Medium Priority
The page creates genuine curiosity that pulls visitors forward through the scroll. Each section reveals something new, making the next section feel like a page turn.
Where on the page: The "What Can I Grow?" quiz creates curiosity about personalised results — visitors want to see what the quiz recommends for their specific situation. The scroll-triggered animations reveal content progressively, creating a sense of discovery. The "Yeah, but..." accordion section uses information gaps (curiosity about the answer) to drive clicks. The micro-stories are structured as mini-narratives with surprising outcomes ("The Kid Who Ate the Kale"). The seasonal timeline invites exploration of different months.
CD8
Loss & Avoidance
Black Hat • Low Priority (Minimal Use)
The lightest-touch core drive on this page. Urban farming should feel empowering, not fear-inducing. Used only through gentle reframing, never guilt or pressure.
Where on the page: The implicit message "Your rooftop is sitting empty right now" creates a gentle sense of missed opportunity without guilt-tripping. The seasonal windows ("Plant now or wait until next spring") introduce natural, honest loss framing. The collective goal progress bar implies the community is moving forward — standing still means being left behind. Critically, this drive is never the primary motivator for any section. It supports urgency at the two conversion points (Herb Pot Challenge and Final CTA) and is immediately followed by White Hat reinforcement.
The Balance: 80% White Hat / 20% Black Hat

This landing page is deliberately designed to be overwhelmingly White Hat. Visitors should feel empowered, inspired, and capable — not anxious, pressured, or guilty. The Black Hat drives (CD6, CD7, CD8) are deployed sparingly and only at natural conversion moments, always followed immediately by White Hat reinforcement.

The primary motivational engines are CD1 (Epic Meaning), CD3 (Creativity & Feedback — the Golden Corner), and CD5 (Social Influence). These three drives ensure that engagement is intrinsically motivated, community-driven, and sustainable — exactly what a community urban farming project needs to grow from 40 rooftops to 200.

People Like You Already Do This

Phibsborough

Niamh bought a €1.99 basil plant from Tesco to make pesto. Instead of letting it die on the kitchen windowsill like usual, she repotted it into a bigger container on her balcony.

It survived. Then it thrived. She started picking leaves every evening for dinner. That single basil plant led to three more pots -- parsley, mint, chives -- then a conversation with her upstairs neighbour who was doing the same thing, then a visit to the Rooftop Republic site on Manor Street. That was April 2025. She now looks after six containers on the building's rooftop and teaches new growers on Saturday mornings. It started with pesto.

Rathmines

Declan's seven-year-old, Roisin, would not eat a green vegetable. Full stop. Non-negotiable. Then the family joined the Rathmines rooftop for a Saturday drop-in.

Roisin planted kale seeds in a yoghurt pot. She watered them every day. She watched them sprout. She named the biggest one Kevin. When Kevin the kale was big enough to pick, she ate it raw on the rooftop and said it was "not disgusting." Declan says it's the single greatest parenting achievement of his life.

Smithfield

Tomasz moved from Lublin to Smithfield in 2006. He'd grown up with his babcia's enormous vegetable garden but hadn't planted anything in twenty years.

When Rooftop Republic started a site on his block, he signed up to be polite. Within a month, he was growing tomatoes, dill, and cucumbers -- the same things his grandmother grew. He started pickling cucumbers on the rooftop and sharing jars with neighbours. The Irish lads call him the Pickle King. He taught three of them how to grow dill. His grandmother would be proud.

Stoneybatter

Margaret had been retired for eighteen months and the days were getting long. She saw a Rooftop Republic flyer in the Stoneybatter post office and nearly didn't go.

"I'd only be in the way," she thought. She went anyway. They gave her a raised bed at hip height so she wouldn't have to kneel (her knee is gone). She grows lettuce, radishes, and herbs. She says the gardening is grand but what she really comes for is the tea and the chat afterwards. She hasn't missed a Saturday since she started.

Yeah, But...

Houseplants are actually harder than outdoor vegetables. Outdoor plants have rain, natural light cycles, and soil biology on their side. You've probably been overwatering your indoor plants in a dark apartment. Outdoor growing is more forgiving than you think. And yes, some things will die. That's not failure -- that's gardening.
Dublin gets about 750mm of rain a year, spread fairly evenly. That's almost perfect for growing leafy greens and herbs. Kale, chard, lettuce, spinach, and herbs thrive in our mild, damp climate. London gardeners are genuinely jealous of our rainfall. The honest limitation: we don't get enough heat for peppers or aubergines outdoors. But your salad and herbs will be magnificent.
In growing season, a small container setup needs 20-30 minutes twice a week. That's watering, checking plants, picking what's ready. In winter, almost nothing. The community meetups are once or twice a month for a couple of hours, and they're optional. This is not a second job.
Container gardens are portable -- you take them when you move. Many landlords are neutral or positive about tenants who improve outdoor spaces. It's worth asking. If the answer is no, start with a windowsill herb garden and join a community rooftop nearby. You don't need permission to grow basil on your kitchen windowsill.
€30-50 gets you started with a few recycled containers, a bag of compost from any garden centre, and a packet of seeds. But honestly? €10 gets you one pot, one bag of compost, and one herb plant. That's enough. Rooftop Republic provides seeds and starter supplies at community events. The most expensive part is if you want proper raised beds later, and you don't need them to start.
That happens to some people and that's absolutely fine. Containers can be passed to a neighbour or to Rooftop Republic for reuse. There's no contract, no obligation. Some people grow for a year and stop. Some come back after a break. Some stay for decades. All of it is fine. You're not signing a mortgage -- you're planting a herb.
🌱 The Herb Pot Challenge

Plant one pot this weekend. That's it. That's the whole first step.

  1. 1 Grab a pot (a saucepan works), fill it with compost (€4 from any garden centre)
  2. 2 Plant a supermarket herb (€2 basil, parsley, or mint) or sprinkle in a packet of seeds
  3. 3 Put it on your windowsill, balcony, or doorstep. Water when the soil feels dry.
€10 total cost
15 min total time
